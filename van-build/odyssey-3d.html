01 · WEIGHT BUDGET

Start with the weight budget, not the wood

A 2009 Odyssey carries roughly 1,300 lb of payload (your exact number is on the driver-door-jamb sticker — use that one). Pulling the 2nd and 3rd row seats gives you back about 180–200 lb. Budget it like this:

That's why the plan says 35 gallons, not 65. A 65-gal tank full is 542 lb of water and eats the whole budget. If you find you need more water per day, refill between jobs — Orcas jobs are close together — or step up to 50 gal (417 lb) only if you strip weight elsewhere. Mount the tank just behind the front seats, centered, so the mass sits between the axles instead of behind the rear one.

02 · THE FLOOR

One plywood deck makes everything else easy

The Odyssey floor is not flat once the seats are out — the 3rd-row "magic seat" tub is a deep well, and the floor is ribbed sheet metal you should never screw into. The fix is a single wood deck that everything else bolts to:

  • Frame the 3rd-row tub with 2×4 sleepers laid on edge so the top of the framing is level with the main floor.
  • Cap the whole cargo area with one sheet of ¾″ exterior-grade plywood (about 78″ × 48″, notched around the wheel wells). Seal it with porch paint or spar urethane — this floor will be wet constantly.
  • Anchor the deck to the factory seat-latch points. The Odyssey's 2nd- and 3rd-row seat strikers/anchors are welded to the frame and rated for crash loads. Bolt the plywood down through them with M8/5⁄16″ hardware and big fender washers, and put a forged eye bolt at four of them, sticking up through the deck. Those four eyes are your tie-down points for the tank straps.
  • Never rely on self-tapping screws into the floor pan — they strip, they leak, and they let go in a hard stop.
03 · WATER

Tank: 35-gal poly leg tank, strapped like it's cargo — because it is

Use a low-profile leg tank (the molded feet have strap grooves; ~38″ × 24″ × 17″ for 35 gal). Place it centered, immediately behind the front seats. Two 2″ ratchet straps run over the strap grooves down to the eye bolts at the seat anchors — rated straps, not bungees. A full tank hitting your seat backs in a 30 mph stop carries tons of force; the factory anchors can take it, screws into plywood alone cannot.

  • Plumb a ¾″ bulkhead fitting low on the rear face → ball valve → 100-mesh inline filter → garden-hose thread to the pressure washer inlet.
  • Add a fill port you can reach through the sliding door, and a vent (a loosely-capped lid works).
  • Half-full tanks slosh hard. Fill full or near-empty when you can, and take corners like you're carrying soup.
04 · THE REEL — YOUR STICKING POINT

The fix for the Giraffe reel

Stop trying to mount it to the van. Build it a wall.

The Giraffe bracket is designed to lag into wall studs — four screws deep into solid lumber. The Odyssey gives you nothing like that: the interior panels are plastic over air, and the body skin is thin sheet metal that will flex, crack the plastic, and eventually tear out. That's why nothing you've tried holds. The answer is to bring the "wall" with you:

  1. Build a mounting wall: two vertical 2×4 studs, 16″ apart, bolted at the bottom through the plywood deck into the rear seat-anchor hardware, with a ¾″ plywood face screwed across them. Tie the top of the wall to the side-wall rib with one bracket if you want zero wiggle, or run a 2×4 header across to a matching post — no holes in the van body needed.
  2. Place it on the driver side, just ahead of the tailgate, facing rearward — the Giraffe pivots 180°, so the hose pays straight out the open hatch and the auto-rewind pulls straight back in. No dragging the hose around door edges.
  3. Through-bolt, don't screw: mount the Giraffe bracket with 5⁄16″ bolts + fender washers + nylock nuts straight through the plywood face. The rewind spring yanks on that bracket every retract; through-bolts never work loose the way wood screws do.
  4. Height check: center the bracket ~34–36″ off the deck so the reel body clears the shelf below and the hose exits level with the tailgate opening.

Total parts: two 2×4s, a 2-ft square of ¾″ ply, four carriage bolts for the base, four through-bolts for the bracket. An afternoon, and the reel will feel like it grew there.

05 · PRESSURE WASHER

Slide-out tray at the tailgate — never run it inside

If the washer is gas-powered, this is non-negotiable: never start it inside or even at the open hatch of the van. Carbon monoxide and a hot muffler in a closed metal box is how conversions burn down. The setup that works:

  • Mount the washer on a 250-lb locking drawer slide tray (or simpler: a plywood tray with a handle) at the rear passenger corner. Slide it out past the bumper, or lift it out and set it on the ground to run.
  • Locking pins or a strap hold it for transport; quick-connects on the water feed and pressure hose make deploy/stow a 60-second job.
  • Electric alternative: a 2,000 PSI electric washer can legitimately run from the tray if the hatch is open, and pairs well with jobs that have an outlet. Many mobile detailers carry gas for driveways, electric for garages.
  • Gas cans ride in a vented plastic tray, never loose.
06 · STORAGE

Wood-framed bin racks down both sides

Yes — a wood frame is the right call, and it's the same trick as the reel wall: the frame bolts to the deck, not to the van.

  • Frame: 2×2 uprights lag-bolted to the plywood deck, ~24″ bays, with ½″ ply shelves at 13″ and 24″ above the deck. Keep racks 12″ deep so the center aisle stays open.
  • Screw a 1×2 lip along every shelf edge and add a bungee across each bay — bins stay put on the ferry ramp and Doe Bay Road alike.
  • Driver side carries chemicals and pads (forward of the reel wall); passenger side carries towels, buckets, and the vacuum, forward of the washer tray.
  • The flat tops of both wheel wells are free real estate — cap each with a ply shelf for the heavy stuff you grab most.
07 · PLUMBING & POWER

Water path and the one wire you need

The whole water system is one line: tank → ball valve → filter → washer inlet, with the washer's own pump doing the work (most 2.5 GPM units gravity-feed fine from a tank sitting higher than the inlet — the tray position in the model keeps the inlet below tank bottom). The Giraffe reel carries your rinse/supply hose. Optional but worth it: a 12V diaphragm pump + accumulator teed off the tank gives you pressure for rinseless washes and bucket filling without starting the washer. Run it off a 50 Ah deep-cycle battery behind the driver's seat with an isolator to the van battery, and it also powers work lights and charges the vacuum.

09 · BUILD ORDER

Weekend-by-weekend

  • Prep: seats out, carpet out (it will never dry), locate all seat-anchor points, measure your actual van — the model uses nominal 3rd-gen Odyssey dimensions, verify before cutting.
  • Deck: frame the seat tub, cut and seal the plywood, bolt it through the seat anchors, install the four eye bolts.
  • Reel wall: build the 2×4 + ply wall, bolt to deck and rear anchors, through-bolt the Giraffe bracket, test pull-out and auto-rewind with the hatch open.
  • Tank: set the leg tank, ratchet-strap to the eyes, install bulkhead → valve → filter line.
  • Washer tray: build slide-out or lift-out tray, add quick-connects, strap point for transport.
  • Racks: 2×2 frames, shelves, lips, bungees; load bins and shake-test on a bumpy road.
  • Shake-down: full tank, drive your bumpiest regular route, re-torque every bolt after the first week.
Dimensions are nominal for a 3rd-gen (2005–2010) Odyssey with 2nd/3rd rows removed — measure your van before cutting. Model is illustrative, not to fabrication tolerance.
